Why Bali's Hidden Beaches Are Worth the Hunt

Everyone knows Kuta. Everyone has heard of Seminyak. But Bali's coastline stretches for hundreds of kilometres, and tucked between the dramatic cliffs, dense jungle, and rice-terrace-lined hills lie secret shores that most visitors never find. These are the beaches where the sand is untouched, the water is crystal-clear, and the only footprints are yours. If you're willing to hike a little further, squeeze through a narrow gorge, or follow an unmarked path down a cliff face, the reward is nothing short of extraordinary.

Here's our guide to Bali's best hidden beaches — the ones the guidebooks skip, the ones the tour buses can't reach, and the ones that will stay with you long after you've left the island.

1. Bias Tugel Beach — Padangbai's Best-Kept Secret

Most travellers passing through Padangbai are only there to catch the ferry to the Gili Islands or Lombok. That's their loss. A short 10-minute hike along a clifftop trail from the main harbour leads you to Bias Tugel — a small, crescent-shaped cove with powdery white sand and some of the clearest blue water on the island. The path isn't signposted well, which keeps the crowds away.

Best time to visit: Early morning before 9am. Bring snorkelling gear — the reef just offshore is alive with parrotfish and sea turtles.

2. Nyang Nyang Beach — A Clifftop Adventure

Getting to Nyang Nyang is part of the experience. From the Uluwatu area, you'll descend a steep dirt path through dry scrubland, navigating roughly 500 steps down to the beach floor. The descent takes about 20 minutes and can be slippery after rain — but what greets you at the bottom is a 2km stretch of wild, wave-washed beach almost entirely to yourself.

The surf here is powerful and the undertow strong, so swimming isn't always safe. But watching waves crash against the cliffs while you walk the empty shoreline with the wind in your hair? That's pure Bali magic.

  • Getting there: Take a scooter to Jalan Nyang Nyang Asri, Pecatu. Look for the trail near the small Hindu temple.
  • Tip: Wear sturdy sandals or shoes — the path is rocky.

3. Green Bowl Beach — Snorkeller's Paradise

Carved into the Bukit Peninsula cliffs, Green Bowl Beach earned its name from the green sea algae that clings to the rocks at low tide. It's accessed via a narrow staircase of 300-plus steps — steep enough to deter casual visitors, which means on most days you'll find only a handful of people here.

The snorkelling is superb when the tide is calm. A series of natural caves dot the cliff face, and during low tide you can explore them on foot. The water ranges from deep turquoise to brilliant aquamarine depending on the light.

Pack food and plenty of water — there are no warungs (food stalls) at the bottom, and climbing back up in the Balinese heat is thirsty work.

4. Pasir Putih (White Sand Beach) — Candidasa's Hidden Gem

Near the sleepy village of Candidasa on Bali's east coast, Pasir Putih sits behind a headland that keeps it hidden from the main road. You'll hire a local boat from the fishing village for a small fee, or take a 15-minute walk along a jungle path — both routes deliver you to a wide arc of white sand backed by coconut palms and fringed by coral reef.

A couple of low-key beach bars serve cold Bintangs and fresh grilled fish. The vibe is relaxed, the snorkelling is excellent, and the sunsets are spectacular. This is the kind of beach where you arrive planning to stay an hour and end up staying all day.

5. Atuh Beach — Nusa Penida's Jaw-Dropping Secret

Nusa Penida has exploded in popularity, but most day-trippers stick to Kelingking, Crystal Bay, and Angel's Billabong. Atuh Beach, tucked into the island's far eastern tip, remains genuinely off the beaten path. A winding, bumpy scooter ride followed by a steep staircase descent brings you to a bay of extraordinary beauty — framed by towering limestone pillars rising straight from the sea, with a small white sand beach and water so clear you can see your shadow on the ocean floor.

There's one small warung here serving nasi goreng and coconut drinks. The locals are friendly and the atmosphere unhurried. If you're visiting Nusa Penida, this is the beach you shouldn't miss — and likely won't share with more than a few others.

6. Gunung Payung Beach — Uluwatu's Hidden Pocket Cove

Fewer than 15 minutes from the famous Uluwatu Temple lies a beach that almost nobody visits. Gunung Payung requires a descent of around 280 steps through a narrow valley, but the cove you find at the bottom feels like a reward earned. The beach is small — maybe 80 metres of sand — flanked by dramatic limestone cliffs with a freshwater spring that trickles down the rock face near the waterline.

The water is exceptionally calm compared to other Bukit beaches, making it one of the few spots in the peninsula where swimming is genuinely safe and enjoyable. Morning light here is extraordinary, and because the beach faces east, you'll catch the best colours in the early hours.

7. Guyangan Waterfall Beach — A Hidden Double Reward

On the northern coast of Nusa Penida, Guyangan combines two of travel's great pleasures: a dramatic cliff-top waterfall and a secluded beach accessed by a 400-step steel staircase bolted into the cliff. The waterfall flows year-round into the ocean below, and the beach at the base is a tiny strip of black and white sand hemmed in by rock walls.

It's more adventurous than scenic — the swim here is in open ocean, not a calm bay — but the drama of the setting is unmatched. Arrive before 10am to have it to yourself.

Tips for Exploring Bali's Hidden Beaches

  • Hire a scooter — most hidden beaches require navigating small roads that tour buses and taxis won't attempt. A scooter gives you freedom and flexibility.
  • Bring cash — small entry fees and parking charges are common, and remote beaches rarely have ATMs nearby.
  • Check tide times — many of Bali's secret coves are only fully accessible (or safest for swimming) at low tide. Check before you go.
  • Pack out what you pack in — these beaches stay pristine because visitors respect them. Keep it that way.
  • Start early — the best lighting, the coolest temperatures, and the fewest people are all found in the first two hours after sunrise.

The Real Bali is Still Out There

Bali's reputation as an overcrowded tourist destination is, in many places, well-deserved. But the island's secret beaches prove that the real Bali — raw, wild, breathtaking — is still very much alive for those willing to venture beyond the main roads. Lace up your shoes, pack your snorkel, and go find it.
